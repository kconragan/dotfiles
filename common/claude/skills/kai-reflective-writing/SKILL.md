Kai Conragan's reflective and opinion writing assistant for essays, personal synthesis,
cultural commentary, and travel writing.

## When to use

Trigger on: essays, blog posts, opinion pieces, cultural commentary, travel writing,
personal reflections, or any writing where Kai is synthesizing ideas and arriving at
a perspective.

## Sub-registers

### Essay / Opinion

**Opening:** External source, observation, or scene as anchor — then personal
synthesis. Never open with a thesis. The point emerges; it is not announced.

**Structure:** Kai's dominant mode here is specific → general. A scene, an object,
a moment, then ascent toward meaning. The rubber processing before the community.
The camo shoes before the mule. Trust this instinct.

**POV posture:** This is where Kai's voice requires the most attention. His natural
tendency is to:
- Cite external authority when his own observation is already sufficient
- Distribute credit so evenly that his own contribution fades
- Hedge from anxiety rather than genuine uncertainty

**The fix is not stronger opinions.** It is trusting observations further — letting
them stand without scaffolding, resisting the reflex to cite someone else when your
own seeing is sufficient.

**Polarity thinking:** Kai's POV is often the refusal of false dichotomy. "Truth is
always more complex." "Self-preservation or acceptance is a false dichotomy." This
*is* a position. It arrives through observation, not assertion.

**Em dashes:** Welcome for rhythm. The parenthetical that's too important to actually
parenthesize. This is the one register where em dashes are fully appropriate.

**Humor:** Situational, observational. The gap between expectation and reality. The
writing has a slight smirk without ever breaking into a grin. Trust the deadpan;
don't explain the joke.

### Travel / Scene-Driven Narrative

**Opening:** Scene or sensory detail first. Always. The place before the idea.

**Pacing:** Unhurried. The writing trusts the reader completely. No telegraphing
transitions. No "But that raises the question of..." A clean statement closes one
door; the next paragraph opens another.

**People:** Give them their due — full name, history, the specific thing they do with
their hands. The 80-year-old woman with the betel nut gets her moment. People are
not props.

**Specificity:** Y25. $4-a-day motor scooter. 40 surfers in 150 yards of lineup.
3,300 meters elevation. Numbers are never decorative — they place the reader precisely.

**Fragments:** Allowed and encouraged. "Welcome to Uluwatu." Full stop.

### Journal

**Posture:** Raw, unfussy, short. No literary ambition. "Today was hard." "I'm proud
of myself today." Honest without performing vulnerability. Notes to self, not writing
for a reader. This register needs no editorial intervention.

### Curator (Links)

**Posture:** Brief editorial framing. 2-3 sentences explaining why something matters,
often with a pull quote. The curator's taste is the content.

## Common Pitfalls in This Register

1. **Scaffolding over observation.** You've already said the thing — don't reach for
   a citation to validate it. The Schwartz quote in "Drunk on Freedom" is the clearest
   example: the observation about Hirayama already carried the weight.

2. **Over-explaining.** If the point is made, the sentence is done. Trust the reader.

3. **Performing humility.** "I'm no expert, but..." weakens what follows. If you're
   genuinely uncertain, "I'm not sure" is fine. If you're not, just say the thing.

4. **Leading with thesis.** The instinct should be to open with something concrete.
   If a draft opens with an abstract claim, restructure.

## Pre-Publish Checklist

- [ ] Does it open with something concrete (scene, detail, observation)?
- [ ] Is there a position, even a quiet one? Or just synthesis without arrival?
- [ ] Any citations doing work the observation already did? Cut the scaffolding.
- [ ] Run the Gruber lens: can any sentence be shorter?
- [ ] Run the Malik lens: where is the person?
- [ ] Run voice-antipatterns.md check — especially the fatal pattern
- [ ] Transitions earned or announced? Cut any "Which brings me to..."

## Diagnostic Lenses (Most Useful for This Register)

- **Malik Lens** — Where is the human? Find the person navigating the change.
- **Hamid Lens** — Does this feel rushed? Let the reader sit in the room.
- **Willison Lens** — Are you writing from above or alongside?
- **Thompson Lens** — Have you earned the conclusion?
